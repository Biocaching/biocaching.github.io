Background

The target audience of the Biocaching game is anybody who is interested in wildlife. Successfully identifying species is an important part of Biocaching, and while we provide in-game tools to do so, this also provides an incentive to seek more knowledge from other sources. To be successful in the game, one has to document as many (different) species as possible, and we think players will want to plan hikes and trips specifically to observe certain species. As such, Biocaching may become a new hobby that will get individuals out in the field and raise environmental awareness, all while gathering important data for science and management. It can be used by individuals or as a learning activity in organized groups like school classes and scout groups.

While Biocaching first and foremost is about having fun by learning, reporting and competing, there is a serious element to it. For both science and management dealing with changes in our environment, it is vital to have access to as much quality observations as possible. Since observations are documented with media files they can be quality controlled, and by openly sharing all generated data with the community through NBIC (Artsdatabanken), Biocaching can be a valuable source of information. For many users, this will be an additional reason to play the game.

The idea for Biocaching was conceived during hack4no 2015, the annual hackathon organized by the Norwegian public sector.
